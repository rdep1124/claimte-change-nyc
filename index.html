Climate Change Crisis Will Only Further Burden New Yorkers in Coming Years, According to Researchers

The summer of 2023 was the hottest summer ever in recorded history. What can New Yorkers expect if these trends continue?

By Ray DePaul

October 12, 2023

The past few months of 2023 showcased climate emergencies after one another; wildfires spanning across New York State, rain storms drowning the city and temperatures way above predicted. Complications that have risen may have been unexpected in their severity, but they are logical consequences of prior action (or lack thereof) and policy taken to tackle climate change. The results have led to concerns for New Yorkers of all different demographics. As seen with the most recent storm in New York in late September, poor infrastructure affects everyone, from those who take limousines and private cars to those riding the subway or the bus.


Fossil fuels are a leading cause of climate change around the world, as the greenhouse gases produced absorb the heat and re-remit it into the Earth’s atmosphere. New York City’s energy sources are incredibly reliant on fossil fuels across all areas of energy usage. These include residential, commercial/institutional and construction, which totaled 15 million metric tons of “Carbon Dioxide equivalent” (MtCO2e). While these high numbers are true for almost all big cities around the world, it is clear that New York City’s energy sources contribute greatly to the climate crisis.


If 2023's Summer is any indication of the future ahead of New Yorkers, what can residents of New York City expect in the foreseeable future?
